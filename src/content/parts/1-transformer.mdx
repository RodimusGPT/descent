The model that answered you is a **transformer**. Strip away the branding and it is a
tall stack of the same simple operations, applied over and over to a list of numbers. Let
us walk down through it, from the words you typed to the single token it chose to say next.

## Tokenization

Before the model can do anything, it chops your text into pieces it recognizes. Those
pieces are **tokens** — not quite words, not quite letters, but **subword** chunks learned
from data. A common word like *the* is usually one token; a rarer or compound word like
*tokenization* splits into several (*token* + *ization*). Spaces and punctuation count too.

Each distinct chunk maps to an integer **id** drawn from a fixed vocabulary of tens of
thousands of entries. From here on, the model never sees your letters — only this sequence
of ids. Type into the box and watch the split change.

## Embeddings

An id is just an arbitrary index; on its own it means nothing. So the model's first move is
to look each id up in a big table and replace it with a **vector** — a list of a few thousand
numbers. This is the **embedding**, and it is where meaning begins.

Tokens with similar meanings get similar vectors, so they sit near each other in this space.
More strikingly, *directions* carry relationships: the step from *king* to *queen* is about
the same step as *man* to *woman*. The scatter below is a flattened 2-D cartoon of that idea —
click a word to see its nearest neighbors.

## The stack

With every token now a vector, the real work begins. The model is a stack of **N identical
layers** — dozens of them in a large model. Each layer does two things in turn: an
**attention** sublayer, where tokens exchange information, and a **feed-forward** sublayer,
which transforms each token on its own.

Two details keep a stack this deep trainable. A **residual connection** wraps each sublayer,
adding its input back to its output so signal (and gradient) has a clear path all the way
down. And **RMSNorm** is applied *before* each sublayer — the "pre-norm" arrangement — to keep
magnitudes stable. The vectors flow up through the stack, getting richer at every layer.

<Figure caption="One pre-norm layer: norm → attention → add, then norm → FFN → add, repeated N times.">
  <TransformerStack client:visible />
</Figure>

<DeeperBlock summary="Pre-norm and RMSNorm">
Pre-norm means the block computes $x + \text{Sublayer}(\text{Norm}(x))$ rather than
$\text{Norm}(x + \text{Sublayer}(x))$ — keeping a clean residual highway that makes very deep
stacks stable to train. RMSNorm rescales a vector by its root-mean-square,
$\text{RMSNorm}(x) = \dfrac{x}{\sqrt{\frac{1}{d}\sum_i x_i^2 + \epsilon}}\odot g$, a cheaper
cousin of LayerNorm that drops the mean-centering.
</DeeperBlock>

## Attention

Attention is the mechanism that makes transformers work. At each layer, **every token looks
at every other token** and decides how much to borrow from each. A pronoun reaches back to
the noun it refers to; a verb pulls in its subject. The weights are *soft* — a token spreads
its attention across many others, more on some, less on others.

Click a token in the fan to make it the one doing the looking. Warmer, thicker lines show
where its attention flows; switch heads to see a completely different pattern of who-attends-
to-whom.

## Queries, keys, values — and many heads

How does a token decide *where* to look? It projects its vector into three smaller ones: a
**query** (what am I looking for?), a **key** (what do I offer?), and a **value** (what will I
hand over?). A token compares its query against every key with a dot product, scales the
result, softmaxes it into weights, and takes the weighted sum of the values.

One such computation is a **head**. A layer runs many heads in parallel, each with its own
projections, so different heads specialize — one tracks syntax, another the previous token. A
modern twist, **GQA/MQA**, lets several query heads *share* one set of keys and values. That
shrinks the **KV cache** we'll meet in Part 3 — remember this; it pays off later.

<Figure wide caption="Q·Kᵀ → scale → softmax → weighted sum of V, per head. Toggle GQA to share K/V across heads.">
  <QKVMultiHead client:visible />
</Figure>

<DeeperBlock summary="The attention equation">
For a head with key dimension $d_k$:
$$\text{Attention}(Q,K,V) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^{\top}}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V.$$
The $\sqrt{d_k}$ divisor stops the dot products from growing with dimension and saturating the
softmax into a near-one-hot spike, which would kill the gradient.
</DeeperBlock>

## The feed-forward network (and Mixture-of-Experts)

Between attention sublayers sits the **feed-forward network**: two big matrix multiplies with
a nonlinearity between them, run on each token independently. This is where **most of a
model's parameters live**, and much of its stored knowledge with them.

**Mixture-of-Experts** replaces that single FFN with many **expert** FFNs plus a small
**router** that, for each token, picks only the top few. So a model can carry an enormous
*total* parameter count while running a small **active** fraction per token — say a few
billion active out of a hundred-plus billion total. More capacity, similar cost per token.

## From logits to a token

At the top of the stack, the final vector is projected back out to the whole vocabulary,
giving one score — a **logit** — for every possible next token. Softmax turns those into
probabilities. But we rarely just take the most likely token; that gets repetitive.

**Temperature** flattens or sharpens the distribution. **Top-k** keeps only the k most likely
candidates. **Top-p** (nucleus) keeps the smallest set that covers probability p. Then we
**sample** one token from what survives. Drag the sliders and watch the distribution reshape.

## Autoregression

Now the loop. Append the chosen token to the sequence and feed the whole thing back in to
predict the next one — then again, and again, **one token at a time**, until the model emits a
stop token or hits a limit. "Generating" is just this same forward pass run over a sequence
that grows by one each step.

Notice the waste: every step re-reads the *entire* context from scratch. The fix — caching
what has already been computed instead of recomputing it — is the whole drama of Part 3.

<Figure caption="Append the sampled token, feed it back, repeat. The context grows by one each step.">
  <Autoregression client:visible />
</Figure>

So that is the model: tokens become vectors, vectors flow through a stack of attention and
feed-forward layers, the top of the stack produces a distribution, and we sample from it on a
loop. But we have been waving our hands at those "vectors" and "parameters." What *are* they,
underneath? Just numbers — billions of them. Time to descend into how they are stored.
